# Ideas Graph

A proposed complement to word-level AI detection tools like Pangram, introduced by [[nate-b-jones]] in his interview with Substack CEO Chris Best (2026-07-22).

## The Core Idea

Strip the sentences from a piece of writing and map what remains:
- **Nodes** = concepts
- **Edges** = relationships between concepts
- **Valence** = whether an idea is treated as promising, dangerous, trivial, or central
- **Omission** = what expected ideas are deliberately left out (absence can change the meaning of everything around it)

Compare that conceptual shape against the distribution frontier LLMs produce by default. The gap reveals intellectual originality — not whether the text passed through a model, but whether the ideas inside it diverge from what a model would generate unprompted.

## Why It Matters

LLMs have "intellectual gravity" — they cluster around a central conceptual distribution. Ask for a formal AI document and you'll get ethics/governance whether you asked for it or not. An Ideas Graph makes this gravity visible. It could show that a piece:
- Introduced an unusual relationship between familiar concepts
- Approached a known idea from an unexpected angle
- Left out something nearly every default answer includes

## The Key Insight

From Nate's writing experience: "The model kept the nodes and erased the edges. The meaning had been in the relationships."

He gave a capable model his 15 commandments for AI-native teams and asked it to expand the piece. It preserved every commandment and explained each one fluently — but destroyed the idea. The model kept the nodes; it erased the edges that made the commandments form a *system* rather than a list.

## Research Context

A [2024 short-story experiment](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adn5290) is the evidence base: AI access improved individual creativity ratings while making stories more similar to one another. Individual reach expands; collective diversity contracts. An Ideas Graph would make that trade-off visible at the level of concepts, not just words.

## Status

Proposed framework, not yet built. Chris Best named it "a Pangram for ideas" at the end of their conversation. Nate describes it as a discovery tool for finding writing at the conceptual edges — where genuine human thinking diverges from the model's averaged default.
