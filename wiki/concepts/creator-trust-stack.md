# Creator Trust Stack

A framework introduced by [[nate-b-jones]] for evaluating any piece of AI-assisted media. It replaces the blunt "was AI used?" question with a five-layer trace of where AI operated in production and where human judgment took over to guarantee the final output.

## Definition

> "The real issue is not was AI used. That question is already really a crude question that isn't useful in most cases. The real issue is trust. What was human? What was synthetic? Who made that judgment? And who is accountable for the final thing that you're watching?"
> — Nate B. Jones, [[2026-06-20-nate-b-jones-daily]]

Nate's starting move is to reject "AI or no AI" as a workable test — he calls it "a bad primitive... too blunt... a light switch." In its place, the Creator Trust Stack asks where in the production stack AI operated, and where a human took over to stand behind the result.

## The Five Questions

The precursor to the stack: "was this made with AI?" is actually five separate questions people mash into one.

1. Was the voice synthetic?
2. Was the face synthetic?
3. Was the script synthetic?
4. Was the idea synthetic?
5. Did a human actually approve and stand behind the final output?

## The Five Layers

- **Layer 1 — Disclosure:** what was synthetic, stated clearly — not buried in a vague footnote. Was the voice cloned? The face generated? The script AI-drafted? The edit AI-assembled?
- **Layer 2 — Provenance:** where the source material came from. Was a voice clone trained on consented recordings? Was an avatar built from authorized footage? Was data scraped, licensed, or owned?
- **Layer 3 — Control:** who had the ability to approve, reject, or change the output. Did the person being cloned control the use of their own likeness?
- **Layer 4 — Judgment:** who actually made the argument and decided what claims were worth making.
- **Layer 5 — Accountability:** if the output is wrong, manipulative, or harmful, who owns that — "the part a lot of us want to skip... the part that matters most."

## Why It Matters

A creator using AI to clean up audio is not the same as a creator secretly replacing themselves with a clone. A company drafting a first version of a training video with AI is not the same as cloning an employee's voice without consent. Same tool, different layer of the stack broken — the stack is what tells you which one happened. Nate demonstrates the bar with a live, disclosed clone of his own voice mid-video, arguing the tooling already clears it for a half-attentive audience today, not in some speculative future.
