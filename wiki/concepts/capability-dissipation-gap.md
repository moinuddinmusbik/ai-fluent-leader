# Capability-Dissipation Gap

A framework introduced by [[nate-b-jones]] to describe the widening distance between what AI can technically do and what any given person, organization, or economy has actually reorganized itself to use.

## Two Scales

**Individual scale:** The distance between how AI *can* be used (as an autonomous agent given a precise specification) and how most people *do* use it (as a chat window getting quick, vague requests). People who close this gap capture outsized, persistent returns. Those who don't keep producing 80% outputs and spending the remainder of their time and cost on manual cleanup.

**Economy-wide scale:** The formal economic gap between what AI can technically do and what the economy has reorganized around AI doing. Widened by four inertia forces: Regulatory (laws lag capability), Organizational (workflows predate the tools), Cultural (norms about what work looks like), and Trust (institutions haven't verified enough to let AI act autonomously).

## Key Implication

Neither the AI doom timeline nor the AI boom timeline correctly models this gap. Doom scenarios (e.g. the Citrini formal model) assume conditions too extreme to hold. Boom scenarios underestimate the friction of organizational inertia. The dissipation gap absorbs the disruption — but unevenly, concentrating rewards at the frontier.

## The Individual Skill Implication

The skill that closes the gap at the individual level is **not prompting** — it is *specification writing* combined with treating AI as an autonomous agent rather than a conversational respondent. A real specification (success criteria, constraints, format, audience) handed to AI-as-agent produces categorically different results from a quick chat request.

## Receipts Cited

- IBM's worst single trading day in 25 years triggered by Anthropic's COBOL blog post (~$200B+ market cap wipe) — cited as dissipation lag, not AI doom
- AI compresses service costs 40–70%; services = ~70% of consumer spending
- Citrini doom scenario conditions formally "too extreme to hold"
