## The Big Idea

Model routing is the right call — and soon it will be every company's call. Nate's central argument: once the routing playbook is universal (and it nearly is), routing creates cost parity, not competitive advantage. The real AI edge is migrating to a layer almost nobody is budgeting for: not which model runs the work, but what work you choose to run. He names this the [[imagination-gap]].

## His Argument / Reasoning Chain

1. **Routing consensus → output convergence.** Same routing discipline + same task list = same cost structure + same work product. Differentiation from routing is self-defeating once the playbook spreads.
2. **Model convergence is a task signal.** A $1 model tied a $9 model in experiment. Nate reads: the task was underdefined, not the models equivalent. Commoditized execution has no ceiling on commoditized tasks.
3. **The $40 experiment (Mitchell Hashimoto).** Hashimoto ran a task no routing table would assign — too experimental, too slow. Cost: $40. Outcome: differentiated results. Lesson: advantage forms in the task selection layer, not the model selection layer.
4. **Two-layer architecture.** Engine = cheapest capable model (open-weights where possible, to remove vendor cost-floor risk). Steering = frontier models for precision and ambition. Steering leverage compounds as engine cost falls. The interesting budget question is not "how cheap is the engine?" but "how precise is the steering?"
5. **Imagination as constraint.** Most organizations pointed AI at the existing to-do list. Emails faster, decks cheaper — but nothing new on the agenda. The gap between leaders who pull ahead and those who don't is not a tools gap; it is an imagination gap about what to put on the agenda.

## The Contrarian Edge

Nate breaks from the consensus in a specific way: he agrees routing is correct, but argues that celebrating it as an AI win misunderstands where advantage will form next. The same convergence that makes cheap models attractive is the mechanism that eliminates differentiation once everyone acts on it. His contrarian claim: the organizations investing hardest in routing optimization are building the fastest road to the same destination as their competitors.

## Named Frameworks / Concepts

- [[imagination-gap]] — The gap between AI leaders who are "asking for things that were not even on the table a year ago" vs. those running the old list faster. The scarcest resource when execution is cheap is the ambition of the ask.
- **Two-layer AI architecture** — Engine (cheapest/open-weights) + Steering (frontier models for precision). Not a named brand but a structural framework Nate uses to organize AI budget allocation.
- **$40 question diagnostic** — One-question organizational test: "What would you attempt if AI execution cost $40?" Blank answers identify the imagination bottleneck.

## What It Means For You (Executive Actions)

1. **Run the $40 question** with each function head this week.
2. **Separate engine from steering** in your AI budget — standardize on cheapest capable model for routine execution; reallocate to frontier for steering tasks.
3. **Commission a "new work" inventory** — initiatives previously too slow/expensive to attempt; run them now with cheap AI execution.
4. **Audit your AI task list** — if every task was also on your pre-AI list, you're optimizing the old agenda, not building a new one.

## Mistake to Avoid

Treating model routing as your AI strategy. Routing is a procurement decision — correct, important, and soon universal. Cost parity ≠ competitive advantage.
