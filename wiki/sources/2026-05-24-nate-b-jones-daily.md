# Why the AI boom is about to hit a wall

**Routine:** [[nate-b-jones]] Daily Leader Briefing | **Date:** 2026-05-24
**YouTube:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Poyi6X7rOwY
**Substack:** https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/p/ai-big-tech-industrial-business

## Summary

Nate reframes AI from a software business into an industrial supply chain. Microsoft’s $190B CapEx commitment and the phrase “capacity constrained” reveal a structural reality: the bottleneck is not compute or algorithms — it’s high bandwidth memory (HBM), advanced packaging (90% of chip die area), power infrastructure, and construction timelines that run 18–24 months. Every hyperscaler is spending $80–90B annually on the same constrained supply base.

The executive implication is that AI vendor contracts are quietly becoming supply contracts. Leaders who treat AI as a commodity software service will be blindsided when capacity events hit. The right response is to build supply assurance into AI procurement, shift budgeting from seats to token consumption, and brief Finance on a capital cycle that runs on physical infrastructure timelines, not SaaS quarterly rhythms.

## Key Takeaways

- “Capacity constrained” means HBM and packaging, not GPU compute — cycles that cannot be compressed
- All hyperscalers are committing the same CapEx simultaneously, creating systemic capacity risk
- AI vendor contracts are now supply contracts in everything but name
- Developers belong in procurement conversations — model choices determine supply chain dependencies
- Token forecasting, not seat licensing, is the correct AI budgeting unit
- Three investment review questions: capacity assurance, utilization economics, 30% constraint contingency
