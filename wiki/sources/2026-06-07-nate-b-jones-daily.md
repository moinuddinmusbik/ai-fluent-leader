# Uber Burned Its Entire AI Budget Early. The Bill Was Trying to Tell Them Something.

## The Big Idea

Uber's AI token bill is not an overspend story. It is the first public proof that agentic AI has crossed from a tool you buy into labor you have to manage — and almost no company has built a system to manage labor it cannot see. The bill is not a verdict on AI. It is diagnostic data on an operating model that was never redesigned for agents.

## Why It Matters Now

This is a June 2026 problem, not a December 2025 problem. By May 2026, 95% of Uber's engineers use AI tools monthly — most in agent-style workflows — and an internal coding agent writes roughly 1,800 code changes per week. Uber did exactly what every board demanded: get serious, put tools into real workflows, find the leverage. Then the cost story broke. When AI adoption finally reaches scale inside an enterprise, the 2025 budget model breaks with it. Uber made that visible first.

## His Argument

- **The easy read is incomplete:** Uber's CTO confirmed the company blew through its entire 2026 AI budget months early. The reflexive response — cap usage, rein in employees — misses what COO Andrew Macdonald actually said: he can see usage, commits, and token spend but "cannot cleanly connect any of it to better features for customers." That gap is the real problem.
- **Token burn = labor you cannot see yet:** When AI moves into agent-style workflows, token spend becomes a proxy for work that your operating model was never designed to track, gate, or route. The bill is not noise. It is information about work the company started before it built the system to run it.
- **Three roles, three threats, one missing system:** The budget owner sees a justification for a layoff they did not want to make; the engineering lead sees a cap that kills the experiments that were working; the IC sees "used too much AI" turned into a performance problem. Same invoice, three warnings, one missing operating model.
- **The cost curve is structural, not cyclical:** Even as price-per-call falls, the work you actually want from frontier models keeps getting more expensive — because agentic tasks plan, retry, and run for hours. Seat-and-license pricing was never built for this.

## Receipts

- **Uber President & COO Andrew Macdonald:** "can see the usage, the commits, and the token spend, and still cannot cleanly connect any of it to better features for customers"

## The Contrarian Edge

- **Consensus says cut the spend.** The comfortable move is to blame adoption velocity and cap token budgets by team.
- **Nate's counter:** "Spending freely and capping hard are both easy, and both are wrong." A hard cap kills the experiments that were working and signals to the organization that the AI ceiling is real.
- **The operating model is the bug, not the bill.** The problem is not that Uber's engineers used too much AI. The problem is that Uber's operating model — built for software tools — cannot produce the accountability signal that connects a token to a customer outcome. That is a management infrastructure problem.

## Framework Named

**[[minimum-effective-intelligence]]** — Nate's routing rule for AI spend: assign each workload to the minimum intelligence tier (frontier model / open model / no model) that meets the task's need. Front-loading this question before work enters a pipeline is the fastest correctable item on any token bill.

## What It Means For You

1. **Read your token bill as a map, not an indictment.** Segment spend: production work, tuition (exploration that builds capability), waste, and signal (workflows worth turning into infrastructure). The distribution tells you where to invest and where to route differently.
2. **Apply [[minimum-effective-intelligence]] before the invoice arrives.** Most tasks do not need a frontier model. Routing open models or simpler tools to non-frontier work is the fastest correctable item on any token bill.
3. **Replace the token cap with a work-object operating model.** Gates, permissions, and accountability need to live at the level of the work — not the dollar. Define what an AI work object looks like, what it outputs, and what approval it needs.
4. **Brief three audiences from one dashboard.** Budget owner, engineering lead, and ICs each read the same token spend as three different threats. One integrated signal — connecting token volume to work objects to customer outcomes — resolves all three without the cap reflex.

## Mistake to Avoid

Treating a rising token bill as evidence that people are using AI irresponsibly. A cap on usage is a cap on the experiments that were working. The bill shows where agentic work is happening; the answer is to route it better and build the operating model it requires.
