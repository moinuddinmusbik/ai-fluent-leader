## The Big Idea

Claude Fable 5 is the first model where the ceiling isn't the model — it's you. For three years, AI broke on your work, so you learned to ask small. Fable 5 has inverted that: the wall Nate kept hitting wasn't the model running out of ability, it was him running out of work large enough to give it. The bottleneck has moved from *what AI can do* to *what you can imagine handing over*. And that, Nate argues, is a skill story, not a capability story.

## Why It Matters Now

Fable 5 was pulled offline by the US government days after launch — so why publish the review anyway? Because this capability isn't staying with Fable. Nate says the "big model feeling" is the leading edge of what lands in the next ChatGPT (weeks away) and open-weight models (4–6 months out). The model will return, or something equally capable will arrive. The skill — knowing how to imagine and hand over whole jobs — is the part worth building right now.

## His Argument

- **Reset 1 — The limit flipped:** Fable 5 is bigger, not just smarter. "Bigger" means it can pick up and carry an entire job. The new constraint is the human's ability to imagine the ask — a complete reversal from the 2023–24 era when models broke at step six.
- **Reset 2 — Three years burned in asking small:** Prompt engineering became a profession because the models were tiny. We got good at small asks, and that skill calcified. Every AI guide, including Nate's own, was written under that assumption. The models scaled; the asks didn't.
- **Reset 3 — "Ask them" → "Give them" ([[detailed-task-imagination]]):** The new core skill is seeing the whole job an AI could do if given the right context, tools, and a clear picture of what done looks like. This is not delegation (tasks already in your tracker) — it's spotting the gnarly, ambiguous, nobody-owns-it work that nobody assigned because it felt too big.
- **Reset 4 — Fable-sized jobs are the painful, unlisted ones:** The qualifying signal: if it makes you sigh and face-palm, it's Fable-sized. Merge 2 million CRM records; fact-check the 500-page board packet; process 40,000 reviews.
- **Reset 5 — The job risk is precise, not general:** Only jobs with "strict execution and zero judgment" are at risk. Fable still needs to be directed, aimed, fed, judged, and reviewed. The new role is *model manager*.

## Receipts

- **Stripe:** Compressed months of engineering work into days using Fable 5.
- **Poisoned-database test:** Nate handed Fable a database deliberately corrupted with ghost records, fake credentials, and planted traps. Fable quarantined the garbage (didn't smooth it over), inventoried fake credentials without leaking them, and — unprompted — built a review queue. "It behaved like it expected to be checked."
- **$50/million output tokens:** At that cost, small asks are economically irrational. "This is not a model I would use to write a slightly better email."
- **Benchmark saturation:** Fable 5 has "already maxed" Nate's benchmarks — he says he'll have to design new ones.
- **~10 trillion parameter estimate:** Nate believes this is a "massive new pre-train" — a view shared by others with early access.

## The Contrarian Edge

- Not smarter — bigger: Benchmark scores tell you nothing about whether you're assigning work at the right scale. The model can be best in the world and still feel useless if your asks are prompt-sized.
- Visual quality is genuinely weak: Clipped PowerPoint headings, charts "a designer would wince at," missed handwritten-image content until explicitly forced. "Bigger doesn't mean it's finished."
- Job-risk reassurance gets the why wrong: It's not "AI needs humans" generically — Fable specifically needs *model managers* who can scope, feed data, and review at scale. That is a different and new skill.
- Government pulling Fable is almost useful: Study [[detailed-task-imagination]] now, distraction-free, before access returns.

## Framework Named

**[[detailed-task-imagination]]** — "the ability to look at your own work and see the whole job that an AI could do if it had the right context and the right tools and a clear picture of what done looks like." Introduced here as the successor to prompt engineering for big-model-class AI (Fable, next-gen ChatGPT, frontier open-weight). See concept page.

## What It Means For You

1. **Write the weather around your work this week.** List the things "hovering like rain clouds" — gnarly, painful, nobody-owns-them items that make you sigh. Those are Fable-sized jobs.
2. **Audit your ask size, not the model.** If frontier AI has felt smaller than the headlines in your day-to-day, the gap is on your side — not the model's.
3. **Build a data pack before you start.** Expect 2–4 hours of prep before handing over any real job. Brief it like a senior colleague, not a prompt.
4. **Start using "model manager" language with your team.** Introduce the vocabulary before this class of model becomes universally available.

## Mistake to Avoid

Confusing model power with model autonomy. "This model still needs to be directed, it still needs to be aimed, it needs to be fed, it needs to be judged." Handing any model of this class a big job without clear scope, clean data, and a review trail is as wasteful as asking it to rewrite an email.
