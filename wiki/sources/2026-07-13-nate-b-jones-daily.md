## The Big Idea

Benchmarks tell you which AI model is smarter. They do not tell you which one fits *you*. With GPT-5.6 Sol, Fable 5, Grok 4.5, and GLM 5.2 all competitive, the leaderboard no longer makes the choice for you — your own work pattern does. Nate's thesis: most serious leaders should be running *both* Sol and Fable, for different jobs, not making a single bet on a winner.

## Why It Matters Now

The gap between leading models is narrowing; the gap between how different people work is not. When Sol, Fable, Grok, and GLM all have credible claims, defaulting to the leaderboard leader is increasingly the wrong heuristic. The model subscription decision is becoming a strategic one that leaders must own deliberately.

## His Argument / Reasoning Chain

1. **Last week's frame was incomplete.** "Start with the job" was Nate's prior week's advice. He now extends it: the job leaves out the person doing the work. Same task, different work pattern → different model.
2. **Two families, not two rankings.** OpenAI's RL bet and Anthropic's pre-training bet produced genuinely different model temperaments — not just different scores. Sol: executes the full instruction, keeps going, integrates with pre-built tools. Fable: finds intent before it is cleanly articulated, excels in ambiguity.
3. **His own daily split as proof-of-concept.** Fable wins his Dingo suite. He opens Sol every morning. Why? His prompting style — long, technical, edges-known, steer-by-correction — is a Sol pattern. When the task is still ambiguous, he routes to Fable as architect (e.g., inside Ringer).
4. **Models → families.** As capability rises, reasons for picking one will drift further apart across people. The leaderboard becomes less signal, not more.
5. **[[model-fit]] operationalizes the thesis.** A free diagnostic at modelfit.natebjones.com maps your work pattern to a model mix and provides "think bigger" prompts. Includes a two-week calibration window as a falsifiability check.

## The Contrarian Edge

Consensus advice: run the benchmark, pick the winner, switch. Nate's break: Sol is definitionally lower on his benchmarks and he still uses it daily. "Smarter" is not the right axis — fit is. More importantly: two people doing the *same job* but prompting differently should pick different models. The person is a variable the leaderboard ignores entirely. Nate also introduces a temporal argument: as models get better, this divergence widens — the leaderboard becomes *less* useful as a selection tool, not more.

## What It Means For Leaders

1. Run the Model Fit diagnostic (modelfit.natebjones.com) and commit to a two-week calibration test
2. Stop making model selection a team-wide mandate — portfolio approach by work pattern outperforms a single decree
3. Audit your actual prompting style (correct-the-output vs. let-it-find-the-intent) — that predicts your model fit
4. Design your harness for portability across model families now — advantage lives in the harness, not the model score

## Mistake to Avoid

Using benchmarks as a proxy for fit. A 93 on Dingo is a real score on a real test — Nate still doesn't open that model first. The leaderboard tells you which model is smarter in the abstract; it tells you nothing about whether you'll produce better work with it.
