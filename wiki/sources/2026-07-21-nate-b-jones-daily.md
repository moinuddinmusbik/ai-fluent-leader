## The Big Idea

Every agent demo shows you the easy part — it clicked send, it booked the meeting. You could always click send. What eats your week is everything that happens *before* the click: the messy folder, the denial letter, the pile of unstructured documents no one was meant to read quickly. The real value is in the bureaucratic mess before the action, not the action itself. Nate's reusable AI rig handles that mess — structures, cites, exports — and then stops. The agent drafts; the human decides.

## His Argument

Nate's reasoning chain:
1. **The demo trap:** Most builder energy goes into the final action (send, book, pay). That is the easy part. The hard part — reading, sorting, assembling unstructured documents — is where the real time drain lives.
2. **The system asymmetry:** Insurers and tax authorities have structure and automation. Individual claimants and taxpayers have piles. This asymmetry is why <1% of denied ACA claims are appealed even though ~1/3 to 1/2 of appeals win.
3. **The chore trap:** Builders solve the first paperwork problem with a one-off agent, hit the next problem, and it "looks like starting from scratch." So ambition dies in the inbox.
4. **The rig rebuttal:** A 9-stage reusable architecture changes the math. Only the nouns change between builds. By the third build, setup is a fraction of the first. This is a compounding system, not a collection of chores.
5. **The hard stop as a governance layer:** "Agent drafts and organizes only. It never sends, files, submits, pays, or signs." This boundary is not a liability disclaimer; it is what makes the rig safe to point at money and health.

## The Framework — [[reusable-agent-rig]]

**Name:** The Reusable Agent Rig

**Structure:**
- 9 fixed stages from context-scope definition → hard stop/handoff
- Two transferable Open Skills: **Context Engineering** (what the agent reads) + **Runbooks** (step-by-step operating procedure)
- Deterministic retrieval instead of vector search for legal/health documents (exact clause citation matters)
- Hard stop built in: the agent never takes the terminal action

**Three proof-run builds:**
1. Email & calendar (training run — low stakes)
2. Insurance denial appeal packet (high stakes, high asymmetry)
3. Tax-year prep packet (same rig, different context pack + output template)

**The flywheel principle:** "Every agent you build should make your next agent cheaper to build." If that ratio is not improving, you are not building a system.

## Receipts

- ~85 million denied in-network ACA claims in 2024 (KFF, independent health-policy research nonprofit)
- <1% of those were appealed by consumers
- Insurer reversal rate on internal appeal: ~1/3
- External independent reviewer reversal rate: ~1/2
- Prior authorization external reversal rate: >80%
- UnitedHealthcare's post-acute-care denial rate more than doubled in 2 years (Senate PSI 2024)
- Senate PSI (2024): Largest Medicare Advantage insurers deployed algorithmic denial tools as denial rates climbed

## The Contrarian Edge

- **Email agents as the wrong ceiling:** Consensus builders stop at inbox automation. Nate argues the folders that cost real money stay closed while builders pat themselves on the back for email agents.
- **Hard stop as the moat:** Industry trend is toward greater agent autonomy (removing the human in the loop). Nate argues the opposite for high-stakes domains: the hard stop IS what makes the rig deployable.
- **Deterministic over approximate:** Field default is RAG/embeddings for document work. Nate deliberately skips vector search for legal/health docs — the exact policy clause is what wins the appeal; similarity search introduces citations that were never there.

## What It Means For You

1. Audit your AI portfolio: does each agent make the next one cheaper to build?
2. Identify one high-stakes paperwork pile in your organization and treat it as Build 2.
3. Write "agent drafts and organizes only; never sends, files, submits, or pays" into your AI governance policy.
4. Run the email training build first to validate the rig before high-stakes deployment.

## Mistake to Avoid

Building bespoke one-off agents per paperwork problem. Each will rot on its own schedule. The rig's transferability is the architectural decision that determines whether your AI investment compounds or decays.
