## The Big Idea

Every AI agent demo stops at email — and for a reason that has nothing to do with capability. The next real problem (a denied insurance claim, a year of tax prep) looks like starting from scratch: new tool, new setup, new trust questions. Nate's argument is that this feeling is a design failure, not an AI limitation. The fix is a [[reusable-agent-rig]] — a nine-step skeleton assembled once on low-stakes email, then pointed, unchanged, at any pile of sensitive documents with a decision that matters. Only the context pack (what goes in) and the cited packet (what comes out) change between jobs. By the third build, setup takes a fraction of the effort — and that ratio keeps improving.

## His Argument / Reasoning

Nate frames the problem as a **standard of construction**: most people build one-off agents — chores, each of which "will rot on its own schedule." The right standard is: **every agent you build should make your next agent cheaper to build.** If that's not happening, you're collecting chores, not building a system.

The Substack preview establishes the asymmetry driving this: insurers have structure + automation (algorithmic denial tools); the consumer or employee has a pile. The reusable rig is the structural response — it puts the same capability on the other side of the fight.

The **one governing rule** is non-negotiable: the agent drafts and organizes only. It never sends, files, submits, pays, or signs. This is not a disclaimer; it is the architectural decision that makes the system trustworthy for high-stakes domains. "By the end of this piece, you will see why the builds get more valuable, not less, because of it."

## The Framework: [[reusable-agent-rig]]

A nine-step reusable rig for high-trust paperwork with these structural properties:

- **Stage 1→N:** From "deciding what the agent is allowed to read" to "a hard stop that hands the work back to you"
- **Between jobs:** Only the nouns change — context pack (input documents + structure rules) and export template (the packet format). The nine steps transfer intact.
- **Training run:** Build 1 on email/calendar. Confirm the skeleton works at no cost before extending it to irreversible decisions.
- **Build 2:** Insurance appeal packet — cited, reviewable, human decides before any submission
- **Build 3:** Tax prep packet — same nine steps, different document pile
- **Underlying skills:** [[context-engineering-open-skill]] (structuring what goes in) + Runbooks (behavioral rules for what the agent does); both as portable SKILL.md open skills
- **The flywheel:** Context packs, export templates, and runbooks all sharpen with use. Each build reduces the marginal cost of the next.

## Receipts

- **KFF (independent health-policy research nonprofit):** ~85 million denied in-network ACA marketplace claims in 2024; fewer than 1% appealed by consumers
- **Reversal rates when appealed:** ~1/3 of internal insurance denials reversed on their own; external independent reviewer: nearly half; prior authorization: exceeds 80%
- **2024 U.S. Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations report:** Largest Medicare Advantage insurers leaned on algorithmic tools as post-acute-care denial rates climbed; UnitedHealthcare's denial rate more than doubled in two years
- **The implication:** Most denials stick not because insurers are right, but because almost nobody files. The asymmetry (structure + automation vs. a pile) is what the rig corrects.

## The Contrarian Edge

The consensus assumption is that each new agent task requires its own custom agent. Nate's direct rebuttal: that's collecting chores. The standard should be: **every agent you build makes your next agent cheaper to build.** He extends this to the "send-capable" debate — the hard stop before submission is not a concession to risk-aversion but the feature that unlocks high-stakes use. A "fully automated" version (agent that submits, files, pays) is less capable in the domain that matters, not more.

## What It Means For You

1. **Audit for system vs. chores:** If each agent is standalone with nothing shared, you have chores. Find the reusable skeleton — context pack + nine-step sequence + export template — and start migrating.
2. **Start on email:** Earn trust at the 101 (low stakes) before extending the same machinery to decisions with real consequences.
3. **Bring KFF data to HR/benefits leaders:** <1% of denials appealed; 1/3–1/2 reversed when they are. Recoverable organizational cost. Frame as employee benefit, not IT project.
4. **Hard-code the human gate:** For any agent touching money, health, or legal standing — final action (submit, pay, sign, file) must stay with a human. By design, not disclaimer.

## Mistake to Avoid

Building or approving a "send-capable" agent for high-trust paperwork. Removing the hard stop before submission collapses the trust boundary that makes the system safe to deploy against real stakes. When it fails — wrong form, outdated policy code, hallucinated precedent — the cost is a denied claim, a late filing, or legal liability, not an awkward email.
