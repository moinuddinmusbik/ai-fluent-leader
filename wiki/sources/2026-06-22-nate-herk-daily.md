## What Happened

- **The "now what" question:** Nate frames this for viewers who've already learned Claude — the AI space "never stops moving," so the tool itself matters less than the underlying skills, because every phase (simple chatbot/automation gigs → AI automation agencies → AI agent builders → today's "Agentic" phase, which Gartner projects at ~$22B in spending in 2026) has already cycled out before.
- **The adoption gap:** McKinsey found ~88% of organizations now use AI somewhere in the business, but only about a third have turned that into real projects — "almost everyone is using AI, but almost nobody is good at AI."
- **The next phase is the AI consultant:** not a new builder flavor, but the person who decides what to build, why, and whether it actually worked. The AI consulting market is projected to pass $64B by 2028; roughly 30% of company AI projects get abandoned, and only ~6% of companies using AI are actually good at it.
- **Two roads in:** the independent AI consultant (Nate's own path — diagnose and build solutions for outside businesses, framed as a long-term partner rather than a scrappy automation shop) versus the in-house AI consultant (becoming the go-to AI person inside one company, e.g. a Chief AI Officer or Director of AI).

## Nate's Take

A consultant is "the person who figures out what's actually wrong, and then figures out how to fix it" — builders are the pharmacist who hands you what you ask for; consultants are the doctor who figures out what you actually need, and the doctor gets paid the real money because clients never know what they need, only what hurts. Whichever of the two roads fits your personality (independent for full ownership of your time and variety; in-house for stability and a steady paycheck), becoming that consultant — diagnose, prescribe, prove it worked — is the natural next step after learning the tool itself.

## His Reasoning (With Receipts)

- **Enterprises are already filling the in-house seat:** IBM's 2026 CEO study found 76% of organizations now have someone in a Chief-AI-Officer-type role, up from 26% just two years ago — though Nate flags the caveat that the survey covered only ~2,000 CEOs at giant, mostly public firms (median revenue ~$5.8B), so the small/midsize market most viewers would consult into is "still very much wide open."
- **The demand-side numbers:** the World Economic Forum projects AI creates ~170M new jobs by 2030, a net gain of ~80M after subtracting displaced roles; roughly half of all US tech job postings already ask for AI skills, a figure that doubled in one year.
- **The pay and promotion data:** PwC analyzed close to a billion job postings and found the pay premium for AI skills more than doubled in a single year, from 25% to 56%; a separate study found nearly two-thirds of employees say they've been passed over for a promotion because their skills were outdated.
- **His own credibility:** Nate started at Goldman Sachs, tried to pitch an internal AI initiative and found the firm "way too slow" with too much change-management friction, then built his own AI agency (True Horizon), scaled it past $100K/month in under a year, exited, and built the AI Automation Society community to 400,000+ members in under two years.

## What It Means For Builders

- **His 4-step roadmap:** (1) Audit your own role through a "constraint" lens — what's actually slowing the team or gating revenue, not just what's repetitive — and write down the specific KPI you'd move by fixing it; (2) take on small projects, scope and build a fix for one item, implement it in just one corner of the business, and turn the result into your first case study; (3) become an expert at solving problems, not just building, by noticing the patterns that repeat across projects; (4) formalize the role by bringing your leadership the evidence and proposing the role yourself — "most of you guys aren't going to find this job posted on LinkedIn."
- **The operating rule underneath it:** constraint first, KPI second, build third — every project needs to target an actual business constraint and a clear number you're trying to move before you start building.
- **Proof beats credentials:** the real hiring question is "why should I hire you over the person who watched the exact same YouTube tutorials?" — the people who win are the ones who can prove they delivered real business results, not the ones who learned the most.
- **The case study — Ailin:** a 15-year email developer laid off at 39 with no AI background. She learned n8n and Claude Code, built in public (two YouTube channels, LinkedIn posts, recorded demos of every build), and when she applied for a Head of AI role at a 15-brand company, the recruiter's only question was "What have you built?" She sent case studies, skipped HR, went straight to the CEO, and got the job — in about a year.

## What To Watch Next

- **SMB/mid-market hiring catch-up:** whether the wide-open small-and-midsize consulting market Nate describes starts closing as more companies follow the enterprise CAIO-hiring trend IBM measured.
- **His in-progress book:** Nate says the "full operating model" behind the constraint-first/KPI-second/build-third framework lives in a book he's currently writing.
- **The "AI consultant" label fading:** Nate predicts the title itself has an expiration date, the same way "Excel accountant" or "internet marketer" stopped being a distinct label once the skill became assumed — eventually it's just "consultant."

## Steal This For Your Org

1. **Run the constraint audit this week.** Have your team list what's actually slowing delivery or gating revenue — not just what's repetitive — and attach a specific KPI to each item before anyone builds anything.
2. **Pilot one fix in one corner of the business.** Pick the top item from that list, scope a small solution, implement it in a single team or process, and document the before/after number as a case study.
3. **Ask every AI-skilled candidate or employee "what have you built?"** Treat a portfolio of shipped, documented work as the real credential — not certifications or years of tenure.
4. **If you already run the business, consult yourself first.** You don't need an outside hire or to become one — apply the same audit-build-prove loop to your own operation, one process at a time.

## Mistake to Avoid

Automating busywork instead of targeting a real constraint. Nate's exact warning: "if you automate something that isn't actually constraining the business, you've just spent a week saving maybe 20 minutes on a task that nobody was even waiting on. Nobody cares as much." Picking the easy, visible automation instead of the thing that's actually gating revenue or speed is why most builders never make the jump to consultant.
