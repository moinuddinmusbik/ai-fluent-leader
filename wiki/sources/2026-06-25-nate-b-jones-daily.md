# 2026-06-25 — Bottleneck Migration: The "What's Expensive Here?" Framework

**Lens:** FRAMEWORK · **Creator:** [[nate-b-jones]] · **Source:** YouTube Short (0:21) + Substack preview  
**Transcript:** Unavailable (Shorts-only day; no long-form episode published)  
**Concept introduced:** [[bottleneck-migration]]

---

## Big Idea

AI didn't eliminate the bottleneck in your work — it *relocated* it. For decades, execution was the scarce resource. Every organizational ritual was built to protect that scarcity. AI made execution nearly free. The new bottlenecks are clarity, ambition, distribution, and relationships. Leaders still running the old rituals are protecting a resource that's no longer expensive.

---

## His Argument / Reasoning

Nate's core diagnostic: every organization runs on an implicit answer to "what's expensive here?" The answer drove every process, every ritual, every incentive structure. The move from expensive-execution to cheap-execution should trigger a complete redesign — not an optimization. He names Agile specifically as an example of a smarter-but-still-wrong assumption set: shorter cycles, faster feedback, *but still assuming engineering is the scarce resource*.

The inversion argument:
- Old: spec → approval → build (spec is cheaper than build)
- New: build → learn → spec (building is now cheaper than speccing)

The new scarce resources: **Clarity** (knowing what's worth making), **Ambition** (vision), **Distribution** (earning trust and attention at scale), **Relationships** (compounding trust that no AI replicates).

---

## Receipts (Evidence Cited)

- **Cursor:** $1M → $500M ARR faster than any SaaS company in history
- **Michael Truell (Cursor CEO):** "taste" > technical ability as the primary value driver
- Direct quote from Substack preview: "Now the meeting takes longer than building the prototype. The PRD takes longer than shipping a working version."
- **Agile named explicitly** as a system that reformed execution management but kept the "execution is expensive" assumption intact

---

## The Contrarian Edge

Mainstream AI discourse tells leaders: "use AI to move faster." Nate's break from consensus: speed on execution is the wrong target. The meta-skill is *what to do fast*, not just doing things fast. Building an AI-accelerated version of your old process is still protecting the wrong bottleneck. The redesign signal from AI isn't "go faster" — it's "the entire sequence of decisions needs to collapse."

Also contrarian on relationships: in a world where everyone is optimizing for AI speed, the people who *compound trust* over time become disproportionately scarce and valuable. That's his thesis for why trust/relationships are now the moat.

---

## Framework Named

**[[bottleneck-migration]]** — The "What's Expensive Here?" Inversion Model.  
Maps: old expensive resource (execution) → what AI made cheap → new expensive resources (clarity, ambition, distribution, relationships) → which old habits to retire as a result.

---

## What It Means For You (Actions)

1. **Ritual Audit:** For each recurring process, ask whether it was designed to protect execution. If yes, it's now overhead — eliminate or compress, redirect to clarity and relationship-building.
2. **Prototype before you plan:** Build the working version before writing the spec. Use AI to make being wrong cheap.
3. **Redeploy freed capacity into compounding trust:** Use hours saved not for more tasks but to show up consistently for people.
4. **Redefine "good management":** From execution efficiency → team having "taste" (clarity, ambition, judgment).

---

## Mistake to Avoid

Using AI to run the same PRD → approval → build cycle 20% faster. That's optimizing the old ritual, not redesigning it. The argument is that the entire sequence needs to collapse, not accelerate.
