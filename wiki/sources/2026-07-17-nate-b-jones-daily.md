## The Big Idea

The model Nate enjoyed using more completed the less important job. He gave Fable and Codex the identical open brief — inspect his real business and decide what deserves automating — and their choices exposed a divide that goes beyond capability benchmarks: the agent you reach for on comfort may be systematically steering you toward the safe, finishable problem rather than the high-leverage, messy one.

## His Argument

- **Operating experience ≠ strategic output:** Codex was a delight to work with — no babysitting, smooth tool use. Fable was a hassle, full of permission dialogues and interruptions. Nate nearly called Codex the winner on UX alone.
- **Codex picked the finishable problem:** A research-to-scripting handoff tool — useful, bounded, completable. But it enters after the harder decision is already made.
- **Fable went upstream:** It found the mess before the pipeline — duplicate ideas, weak coverage, instinctive story selection. It attacked the problem of *which story is worth telling at all* — which Nate confirms is one of the hardest jobs in his business.
- **The key question:** "We have become much better at telling models what to do. What happens when we ask them what deserves doing?" Model routing now needs to happen upstream of the deliverable.
- **Why he killed the "magic button":** An early version let the AI pick the problem and build it in one pass. He rebuilt it to stop short after discovery — human judgment must sit between "here are five evidenced offers" and "build this one."

## Receipts

- Live test on Nate's actual business: real files, Slack threads, content workflow
- **Codex's pick:** Research-and-evidence handoff automation (within the existing pipeline)
- **Fable's pick:** Story-selection intelligence (upstream of the pipeline — the editorial judgment layer)
- **The rebuilt skill:** Returns up to 5 evidenced automation offers; stops before building; requires human selection
- **"Big model smell"** (Nate's term at 03:10): recognizing that a model is reasoning about context, not just executing instructions — the signal that separates strategic-discovery behavior from execution-only behavior

## The Contrarian Edge

- **Harness preference is a strategic trap:** The consensus workflow optimizes for the agent that feels best to use. Nate's result suggests comfort may correlate with choosing safer, lower-stakes problems.
- **One experiment ≠ durable model difference:** Nate resists the clean Fable-wins narrative. Could be a harness difference, a lucky run, or both — he refuses to let one test set routing policy.
- **Strategic discovery precedes execution:** The prevailing framing is pick your automation, then pick your agent. Nate argues the sequence should flip: let the agent help decide what's worth automating first.

## Framework Named

**[[strategic-discovery-vs-execution]]** — Nate's framing distinguishing models that identify high-leverage problems (strategic discovery) from models that execute well-defined tasks (execution). Chapter 06:20 of the video. The operative distinction is whether an agent can reason about *what deserves doing* before being handed *what to do*.

## What It Means For You

1. **Run a problem-discovery pass before scoping automation.** Give agents an open brief first; judge them on what problems they surface, not just execution quality.
2. **Separate harness preference from strategic selection.** Track which agent surfaces higher-leverage problems across 3–5 same-brief tests before drawing routing conclusions.
3. **Build a "stop and propose" checkpoint.** Cap agent autonomy at discovery; require human approval before the build step.
4. **Ask for five offers, not one answer.** Design discovery prompts to return multiple evidenced candidates — a ranked shortlist forces engagement with tradeoffs.

## Mistake to Avoid

Letting the agent that's easiest to work with become the default for strategic decisions. Smooth operating experience is a workflow quality — it tells you nothing about whether the model is finding the right problem.
