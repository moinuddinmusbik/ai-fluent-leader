## The Big Idea

The model Nate enjoyed using more completed the less important job. He gave Fable and Codex the identical open brief — inspect his real business and decide what deserves automating — and their choices exposed a divide that goes beyond capability benchmarks: the agent you reach for on comfort may be systematically steering you toward the safe, finishable problem rather than the high-leverage, messy one.

## Why It Matters Now

Most leaders are still using AI as a task-executor: hand it a defined problem, judge it on delivery speed. Nate's experiment lands at the moment when models are capable enough to choose the problem — which means the strategic question shifts upstream. If you're only evaluating agents on how well they execute the tasks you define, you're missing the leverage point: which agent can tell you which task matters?

## His Argument

- **Operating experience ≠ strategic output:** Codex was a delight; Fable was a hassle. Nate nearly called Codex the winner on UX alone.
- **Codex picked the finishable problem:** Research-to-scripting handoff automation — useful, but enters after the harder decision is made.
- **Fable went upstream:** Found the story-selection mess before the pipeline — the highest-leverage problem in the business.
- **The key question:** "What happens when we ask [models] what deserves doing?" Model routing moves upstream of the deliverable.
- **Why he killed the "magic button":** Rebuilt the skill to stop at discovery; human judgment sits between five evidenced offers and the build step.

## Receipts

- Live test on Nate's actual business (real files, Slack, content workflow)
- Codex's pick: Research-and-evidence handoff tool (within pipeline)
- Fable's pick: Story-selection intelligence (upstream of pipeline)
- Rebuilt skill: returns up to 5 evidenced automation offers, stops before building
- "Big model smell" (Nate's term, 03:10): recognizing context-reasoning vs. instruction-execution

## The Contrarian Edge

- Harness preference is a strategic trap — comfort ≠ strategic superiority
- One experiment can't prove a durable model difference; Nate holds this open
- Strategic discovery precedes execution — flip the sequence: discover what's worth automating before defining the task

## What It Means For You

1. **Run a problem-discovery pass before scoping automation.** Judge agents on what problems they surface, not just execution.
2. **Separate harness preference from strategic selection.** Track which agent surfaces higher-leverage problems across 3–5 tests.
3. **Build a "stop and propose" checkpoint.** Cap autonomy at discovery; require human approval before the build step.
4. **Ask for five offers, not one answer.** Ranked shortlist forces engagement with tradeoffs.

## Mistake to Avoid

Letting the easiest-to-use agent become the default for strategic decisions. Smooth UX tells you nothing about whether the model is finding the right problem.
