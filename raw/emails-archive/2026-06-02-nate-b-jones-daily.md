    <tr><td style="padding:28px 36px 20px;border-bottom:1px solid rgb(238,238,238)">
      <h2 style="margin:0 0 12px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(26,26,46)">Core Message</h2>
      <p style="margin:0;font-size:16px;line-height:1.7;color:rgb(34,34,34)">AI raised per-person output by an order of magnitude and reduced coordination costs by exactly zero dollars. The n(n&#8722;1)/2 formula — which calculates how many communication channels exist in a team — now works against large teams with punishing efficiency: when each person theoretically produces at $2M/year scale, the overhead of managing a sixth team member costs millions in lost productivity. The leaders who internalize this will restructure around four- to five-person strike teams and aim higher; those who don't will keep calling meetings to coordinate work that was never structured to be coordinated.</p>
    </td></tr>
    <tr><td style="padding:24px 36px 20px;border-bottom:1px solid rgb(238,238,238)">
      <h2 style="margin:0 0 14px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(26,26,46)">What He Covers</h2>
      <ul style="margin:0;padding-left:20px;font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;color:rgb(51,51,51)">
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>The coordination cost paradox:</strong> AI raised output-per-person by an order of magnitude but left every communication channel at full cost. n(n&#8722;1)/2 is why a 6-person team has 15 links that must all be managed — AI didn't touch a single one of them.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Correctness over volume:</strong> The bottleneck has shifted from how much gets produced to whether what gets produced is correct. Teams optimizing for output volume feel productive while shipping things that don't work — a trap that grows deadlier as AI accelerates throughput.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Scout vs. strike team framework:</strong> Every initiative maps to one of two structural modes. Scouts explore uncertain terrain with wide latitude; strike teams execute defined missions with tight coordination. Mis-classifying them is a leading cause of AI-era project failure.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>The Steinberger Threshold:</strong> The dividing line between people who direct AI agents and those who get directed by them. This is the new talent segmentation line — and the only way to find out which people you actually have is to run a scout mission designed to reveal it.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Expand ambition, not cut headcount:</strong> The structural insight is not "do the same work with fewer people." It is "do dramatically more ambitious work with the same five people." Cutting headcount before restructuring simply produces smaller, still-poorly-organized teams.</li>
      </ul>
    </td></tr>
    <tr><td style="padding:24px 36px 20px;border-bottom:1px solid rgb(238,238,238);background:rgb(248,249,255)">
      <h2 style="margin:0 0 14px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(26,26,46)">As an AI-Fluent Leader — What To Do This Week</h2>
      <ol style="margin:0;padding-left:20px;font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;color:rgb(51,51,51)">
        <li style="margin-bottom:12px"><strong>Run the n(n&#8722;1)/2 audit on your largest team.</strong> Count members, calculate channels (n&#215;(n&#8722;1)&#247;2), then model the same work split across two strike teams of 4&#8211;5. The difference in communication overhead is your structural efficiency case — bring it to leadership this week.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:12px"><strong>Classify every active project as Scout or Strike Team.</strong> For each initiative ask: "Is the mission defined well enough for tight execution, or does it require exploratory judgment?" This single question restructures your staffing and resource allocation decisions immediately.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:12px"><strong>Run a Steinberger Threshold diagnostic on your key talent.</strong> Assign a task that requires directing AI agents to produce <em>correct</em> output — not just high-volume output. Observe who stays in control and who defers. This reveals your actual AI-fluent bench, independent of job title or seniority.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:12px"><strong>Reframe your next headcount conversation.</strong> Before approving or requesting any new hire, apply the test: "Does this expand mission scope, or does it compensate for coordination overhead?" If the answer is the latter, the right fix is structural restructuring, not additional headcount.</li>
      </ol>
    </td></tr>
    <tr><td style="padding:24px 36px 20px;border-bottom:1px solid rgb(238,238,238)">
      <h2 style="margin:0 0 12px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(192,57,43)">Mistake to Avoid</h2>
      <p style="margin:0;font-size:15px;line-height:1.7;color:rgb(51,51,51)">Don't use AI productivity gains as the justification to cut headcount before restructuring. Leaders who reduce team size without first implementing the scout/strike team framework simply produce smaller, still-poorly-structured teams — and lose the high-judgment talent needed to run the model at all. The structural change must come first: classify work modes, cap teams at 4&#8211;5, redirect freed capacity toward higher-ambition missions. Cutting before restructuring optimizes for the wrong variable and compounds the correctness problem Nate identifies as the real bottleneck.</p>
