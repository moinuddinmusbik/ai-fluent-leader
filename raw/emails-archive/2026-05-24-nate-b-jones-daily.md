## Core Message

AI is no longer a software business with a fancy backend — it is an industrial supply chain with physical constraints that compound. Microsoft’s $190B CapEx and the phrase “capacity constrained” signal that AI vendor contracts are becoming supply contracts. The real binding constraint is high bandwidth memory, advanced packaging, and construction timelines that run 18+ months. For AI-fluent leaders, this changes how you buy, budget, and contract for AI at scale.

## What He Covers

- **Why “capacity constrained” means HBM, not GPUs:** 90% of chip die is packaging vs 12% logic — running on 18-month cycles no hyperscaler can compress.
- **Software contracts became supply contracts:** Every hyperscaler is committing $80–90B annually; capacity allocation becomes a strategic lever your vendor controls.
- **Why developers belong in procurement:** Engineering model choices determine which supply chain you depend on.
- **Forecast tokens, not seats:** Token consumption at scale is the correct AI budgeting unit.
- **Three investment review questions:** Capacity assurance, utilization economics, 30% constraint contingency.

## As an AI-Fluent Leader — What To Do This Week

1. **Audit AI contracts for allocation risk.** Find “best effort” or “capacity constrained” language.
2. **Shift budget conversations from seats to tokens.** Model consumption at current growth and 2× growth.
3. **Map your top 3 AI dependencies.** Run a supply assurance exercise for each.
4. **Brief your CFO on the capital cycle.** AI CapEx runs 18–24 months, not quarterly SaaS cycles.

## Mistake to Avoid

Assuming falling token prices mean falling supply risk. HBM, packaging, power, and cooling operate on physical cycles. A supply shock can spike prices and delay capacity in ways no SLA protects against.
