    <!-- THE BIG IDEA -->
    <tr><td style="padding:24px 36px 20px;border-bottom:1px solid rgb(238,238,238)">
      <h2 style="margin:0 0 12px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(26,26,46)">The Big Idea</h2>
      <p style="margin:0;font-size:16px;line-height:1.7;color:rgb(34,34,34)">The gap between AI-native companies like Anthropic and OpenAI and everyone else is not about AI quality — it is about what they have moved out of meetings and documents and into code. Nate's central claim: in the AI era, the operator's job is to shift the company's repeatable coordination into software that agents can act on. When every decision still has to be reexplained by a person, the humans become the rate limit on the business. The 15 Commandments of AI-Native Speed are the operating system for that shift.</p>
    </td></tr>

    <!-- THE FRAMEWORK -->
    <tr><td style="padding:24px 36px 20px;border-bottom:1px solid rgb(238,238,238)">
      <h2 style="margin:0 0 12px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(26,26,46)">The Framework — 15 Commandments of AI-Native Speed</h2>
      <p style="margin:0 0 14px;font-size:15px;line-height:1.7;color:rgb(51,51,51)">Nate wrote 15 commandments in "thou shalt not" language — deliberately severe rules organized into four clusters that together form one operating system, not a menu. He emphasizes: copying a single commandment without the rest breaks the system.</p>
      <ul style="margin:0;padding-left:20px;font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;color:rgb(51,51,51)">
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Speed cluster (C1, C3–6):</strong> Thou shalt not slow down engineering; product does not make roadmaps; product does not control engineering time; product shall be in code daily; product shall sit with engineering daily. Speed is not working more with AI — it is about accelerating learning loops driven by what you can put in front of customers.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Time/meetings cluster (C2, C7):</strong> No meeting longer than one hour; kill monthly meetings. Audit every regular time commitment — allocate the freed hours toward building, because the leverage is extraordinary right now.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Systems/documentation cluster (C11, C12):</strong> Documentation as code — literally, documents that agents can read and act on for standards, hierarchy, and permissions. Design moves into the code, the SDK, and the UI. A rule that lives only in the head of your longest-tenured director does not exist as far as your stack is concerned.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Human cluster (C13–15):</strong> Stay flexible (adapt like water over stone); assume best intent; build with teams. Enduring businesses require small, well-coordinated teams that catch mistakes and clarify intent for AI. C15 — the teaching commandment — means helping others who aren't going as fast, because partial adoption fails when the culture hasn't moved.</li>
      </ul>
    </td></tr>

    <!-- HOW IT WORKS -->
    <tr><td style="padding:24px 36px 20px;border-bottom:1px solid rgb(238,238,238)">
      <h2 style="margin:0 0 12px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(26,26,46)">How It Works</h2>
      <ul style="margin:0;padding-left:20px;font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;color:rgb(51,51,51)">
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>The replacement rule:</strong> You can't kill the roadmap (C3) without simultaneously having product in code daily (C4) and product sitting with engineering daily (C6) — those two commandments absorb the coordination job the roadmap was doing. Strip out the ban without its replacement and you have deleted organizational coordination.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>The photography analogy:</strong> When film cost money, a family took 24 pictures on vacation because scarcity forced choices. Digital photography removed the price barrier; now you have 40,000 pictures and no idea where your kid's birthday went. AI has done the same to all work — the cost of another draft, prototype, analysis, or piece of code is collapsing to zero. But the choices didn't disappear; they just became easier to avoid.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>The enforcement ladder:</strong> From the Substack — a value, a rule, a runtime check, and a human appeal do different work and deserve different owners. The five-rung ladder runs: value → instruction → reminder → hard block → human-owned decision. Nate has already built this as software: a calendar check, a Slack challenge, a deadline, and a human appeal — the machine asks the question, the person keeps the judgment.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Agent legibility requirement:</strong> Any rule that lives only in verbal norms or in someone's head is invisible to your AI stack. Agents act on written context. Moving documentation into agent-readable code is what separates companies that can run at AI speed from those that can't.</li>
      </ul>
    </td></tr>

        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Any team shipping AI features:</strong> The commandments target teams that have adopted AI tools but haven't moved from tool adoption to AI-native speed — because the organizational rules still assume the old scarcity (expensive engineering time, slow context transfer).</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>Leadership operating models:</strong> Especially relevant for product, engineering, and design leaders whose roles were defined by the old coordination structure — product-managed-by-roadmap, design-as-handoff, engineering-gate-kept-by-tickets.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:10px"><strong>The rule everyone resents:</strong> Nate's worksheet: take the rule that makes people sigh in the all-hands. Name the behavior, find the scarcity that created it, ask whether that scarcity still exists, write something a person can tell you broke. That is the method for rewriting your own commandments.</li>
      </ul>

    <!-- WHAT IT MEANS FOR YOU -->
    <tr><td style="padding:24px 36px 20px;border-bottom:1px solid rgb(238,238,238);background:rgb(248,249,255)">
      <h2 style="margin:0 0 14px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(26,26,46)">What It Means For You</h2>
      <ol style="margin:0;padding-left:20px;font-size:15px;line-height:1.8;color:rgb(51,51,51)">
        <li style="margin-bottom:12px"><strong>Run the scarcity audit this week.</strong> List every recurring coordination ritual — meetings, approval chains, roadmap reviews, handoffs — and ask: which scarcity originally created this rule? Engineering time? Slow context transfer? High cost of mistakes? If that scarcity has collapsed, the rule is now running on inertia, not logic. Start with the one that makes your team sigh.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:12px"><strong>Make your rules agent-legible.</strong> Pick one implicit standard (a quality bar, a permissions model, a review process) that lives only in the head of your most experienced person. Write it down in a form an agent can act on. That single act closes the gap between your declared culture and your deployed stack.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:12px"><strong>Don't ban the roadmap without replacing what it does.</strong> If you kill the roadmap before you have product in the terminal daily and sitting with engineering daily, you've removed coordination without replacement. Sequence matters: install the replacements first, then remove the old mechanism.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom:12px"><strong>Treat C15 (the teaching commandment) as a risk mitigation.</strong> The two ways partial adoption fails, per Nate: your team moves fast but the culture and hiring don't catch up; or you pick individual commandments as isolated changes without the system supporting them. The teaching commandment is how you close both gaps — build internal AI literacy as fast as you build AI features.</li>
      </ol>
    </td></tr>

      <h2 style="margin:0 0 12px;font-size:15px;text-transform:uppercase;letter-spacing:1px;color:rgb(192,57,43)">Mistake to Avoid</h2>
      <p style="margin:0;font-size:15px;line-height:1.7;color:rgb(51,51,51)">Copying somebody else's constitution — or extracting one commandment from this system. Nate's explicit warning: killing roadmaps only works because two other commandments absorb the coordination job the roadmap was doing. Copy the ban by itself and you have deleted what used to coordinate your company. The same trap applies to copying Nate's 15 commandments wholesale: they were written for his organization; you need to run the five-question worksheet on your own rules and write your own version, because a constitution that enforces nothing is worse than none.</p>
