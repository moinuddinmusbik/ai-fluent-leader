## The Big Idea

Model routing — sending requests to cheaper models to cut inference costs — is the right call. Nate's argument is that it is also rapidly becoming *everyone's* call: the playbook is public, the consultants all sell the same one, and Fable 5's pricing ($10 per million input tokens, $50 per million output) has made the "do we have to pay for this?" meeting universal. Once every organization on earth has the same routing discipline, routing is no longer an edge. The real AI advantage is migrating to a layer almost nobody is budgeting for: not which model runs the work, but which work you choose to run.

## Why It Matters Now

Fable 5 arrived at double Opus 4.8's output price with frontier capability, turning every leadership meeting into a cost-routing conversation simultaneously. That synchronized reaction means the routing advantage window is short: when the playbook is public and every consultant sells it, the efficiency gain gets arbitraged to zero across the industry in months, not years. The leaders who treat routing as their AI strategy will look up to find they've achieved cost parity with everyone else — not competitive separation.

## His Argument

- **Routing consensus = output convergence:** When every org runs the same routing discipline against the same task list, they reach the same cost structure and the same outputs.
- **Model convergence is a task signal, not a model signal:** A $1 model tied a $9 model in a recent experiment — the task was underdefined, not the models equivalent.
- **The $40 experiment (Mitchell Hashimoto):** Hashimoto ran a task outside any routing optimization table at $40 and produced differentiated results. Advantage forms in task selection, not model selection.
- **Two-layer architecture:** Engine = cheapest capable model / open-weights; Steering = frontier models for precision and ambition. Steering leverage compounds as engine cost falls.
- **Imagination is the constraint:** Most orgs pointed AI at the existing to-do list. The emails got written faster; the decks got built cheaper. Nothing on the agenda changed. The gap is an imagination gap, not a tools gap.

## Receipts

- **Fable 5 pricing:** $10/M input, $50/M output — the trigger for the synchronized routing meeting
- **$1 model vs. $9 model:** Tied experiment result; task-level signal, not model-level
- **Mitchell Hashimoto's $40 job:** Differentiated output from a task no routing table would assign
- **$40 diagnostic:** "What would you attempt if AI execution cost $40?" — names the org's real bottleneck

## The Contrarian Edge

- The routing consensus is a trap: it eliminates differentiation as effectively as it eliminates cost
- "Most people bought AI and then pointed it at the exact same to-do list they had two years ago" — optimization on a shrinking moat
- Durable advantage sits in organizational imagination — a capacity no AI provider or consultant can sell

## What It Means For You

1. **Run the $40 question this week.** Blank answers identify your imagination bottleneck.
2. **Separate engine from steering in your AI budget.** Standardize on cheapest capable model for routine work; allocate frontier budget to steering tasks only.
3. **Commission a "new work" inventory.** Identify initiatives previously too slow or expensive to attempt. Run them now.
4. **Audit your AI task list.** If every task was also on your pre-AI list, you are optimizing the old agenda.

## Mistake to Avoid

Treating model routing as your AI strategy. Routing is a procurement decision — correct, important, and soon universal. Cost parity is not competitive advantage.
